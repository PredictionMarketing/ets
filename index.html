🔧 2. Service Lines — Deep Analysis

2A. Vehicle Forensics / Crash Data Retrieval (CDR)

Their primary advertised service. They claim to extract and analyze Event Data Recorder (EDR) data from vehicles involved in accidents.

Source: etsintelligence.com/vehicle-forensics

What They Claim:

  • CDR data extraction and analysis from vehicle "black boxes"
  • Pre-crash data: speed, throttle, brake, steering input
  • Crash data: impact forces, velocity changes, airbag/seatbelt deployment
  • Post-crash data: duration, secondary impacts, system status
  • Expert testimony capabilities
  • "Certified CDR technicians" on staff
  • Work with attorneys, insurance companies, and law enforcement

How They Position It:

They frame CDR work as the objective, data-driven alternative to traditional accident reconstruction. Their messaging emphasizes that EDR data "doesn't lie" — positioning it as unimpeachable truth in disputed claims. This targets attorneys, insurance adjusters, and fleet managers.

🚩 Red Flag: Their website does not specify which certifications their analysts hold (Bosch CDR Technician? CDR Analyst? Collision Safety Institute?). Legitimate firms prominently display specific credential names and numbers. The phrasing "certified CDR technicians" is technically vague — certified by whom?

2B. Digital Forensics

They advertise cell phone forensics, computer forensics, and data recovery services.

Source: etsintelligence.com/digital-forensics

What They Claim:

  • Mobile device forensics
  • Computer forensics

  • Infotainment & Vehicle System Forensics — GPS routes, call histories, paired device logs

ETS Intelligence's Verifiable Certifications:

🟡 Inconclusive

  • Website references CDR capability but does not prominently display specific certification numbers, Bosch credentials, or ACTAR accreditation
  • No individual analyst names with verifiable credential trails found in public searches
  • They do NOT appear in the Bosch CDR certified provider directory (or if they do, not under the "ETS Intelligence" name)
  • No ACTAR accreditation found linked to the company name
  • They do reference using Bosch CDR tools for data extraction, which is the right equipment
  • They claim capability across multiple vehicle makes and models, consistent with how Bosch CDR tools work
🚩 Critical Gap: ETS Intelligence does not appear in Bosch's publicly searchable certified analyst database. Their website references CDR capability but does not name specific Bosch certifications, course completion dates, or analyst credential numbers. This is a significant credibility gap in the CDR industry where verifiable certification is expected. Saying "certified" without specifying the certification is a common credibility inflation tactic.

What a Credible CDR Firm Looks Like:

  • Names specific analysts with credential numbers
  • Lists Bosch CDR course completions (Level 1, Level 2, etc.)
  • Shows ACTAR accreditation number if doing reconstruction
  • Provides CV/resume of expert witnesses on the website
  • References peer-reviewed publications or conference presentations (SAE, etc.)

Verification Resources:

  • ACTAR Accredited Member Search
  • Bosch CDR Training Info

⚖️ 4. Expert Testimony & Litigation Footprint

Search Results for Court Appearances:

🔎 Finding: Searches for "ETS Intelligence" in combination with court records, depositions, Daubert challenges, and expert witness databases returned minimal to no results. This is notable because:
  • Firms that regularly provide expert testimony develop a searchable court record
  • Expert witness directories (SEAK, ExpertPages, JurisPro, ForensisGroup) did not return ETS Intelligence listings
  • No Daubert challenge records found (which would indicate their methodology has been tested in court)
  • No cases found in public court databases (PACER, state court records) where "ETS Intelligence" or its principals appeared as named expert witnesses
  • No published case law referencing their analysis or testimony
🚩 Red Flag: A company advertising expert testimony services to attorneys should have a verifiable litigation history. The absence of any court record footprint suggests either (a) they are very new to testimony work, (b) they operate under individual names rather than the company name, or (c) the testimony claims are aspirational rather than established.

📊 9. Final Verdict & Strategic Recommendations

Overall Credibility Score: 4/10

ETS Intelligence presents as a small, low-visibility forensics shop that may do competent work for their existing client base but has not built the public credibility infrastructure expected of a leading forensics firm. Their marketing says "expert" but their public footprint says "small vendor."

  • Technical vocabulary is correct — they understand the industry language
  • Service awareness is real — they know what the market needs
  • But credibility is unverifiable from public information alone
  • No named experts, no certifiable credentials, no courtroom track record
